If \(H_A\) is of the form \(p\neq p_0\) then things are more complicated. We still let \(X\) be the normal variable with mean \(\mu_{\hat{p}}\) and standard deviation \(SE_{\hat{p}}\text{.}\) But in this case, extremal means at least as far to the left or the right of \(p\text{.}\) So we can compute the \(p\)-value via
\begin{equation*} p\text{-value}=P(|X-p_0|> |\hat{p}-p_0|)=P(X>p_0+|p_0-\hat{p}|)+P(X\lt p_0-|p_0-\hat{p}|). \end{equation*}
Equivalently, we can find the corresponding \(z\)-score of \(\hat{p}\) and find the probability that \(P(|Z|> |z_{\hat{p}}|)=P(Z> |z_{\hat{p}}|)+P(Z\lt -|z_{\hat{p}}|)\) for the standard normal variable \(Z\text{:}\) It's also worth noting that for either of these tails, \(P(X>p_0+|\hat{p}-p_0|)=P(X\lt p_0-|\hat{p}-p_0|)\) and \(P(Z> |z_{\hat{p}}|)=P(Z\lt -|z_{\hat{p}}|)\text{.}\) So if you find one of these tails, you can double it to find the sum of both tails.
